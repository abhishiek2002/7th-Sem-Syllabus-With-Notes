# **8. Characteristics & Damage Potential of Natural Hazards**

*(Importance: Very High — examiners use this to test understanding of different hazards.)*

You are expected to explain **how natural hazards behave** and **why they cause different levels of damage**.

Most students write random examples. That won’t cut it.
You need clear characteristics + the kind of damage each hazard creates.

---

# **A. Core Characteristics of Natural Hazards**

These apply across most hazards in general.
Use these when the question is broad.

---

## **1. Sudden or Gradual Onset**

* Earthquakes, tsunamis → sudden
* Drought, heatwaves → slow

This affects evacuation capability.

---

## **2. Predictability Level (High–Low)**

* Cyclones: high predictability
* Floods: moderate
* Earthquakes: almost zero

Predictability = better preparedness = lower casualties.

---

## **3. Frequency Variation**

Hazards have different recurrence intervals:

* Annual floods (Assam, Bihar) → high frequency
* Major quakes (Himalayan belt) → low frequency

Frequency helps classify risk.

---

## **4. Spatial Distribution**

Different hazards occur in specific regions:

* Cyclones → coastal
* Earthquakes → seismic zones
* Drought → arid/semi-arid

This helps disaster planning.

---

## **5. Intensity & Magnitude**

Measured through:

* Richter Scale (earthquakes)
* Saffir-Simpson scale (cyclones)
* Heat index (heatwaves)
* Rainfall intensity (floods)

Intensity directly affects damage.

---

## **6. Duration of Impact**

* Earthquake: seconds
* Cyclone: hours
* Flood: days to weeks
* Drought: months

Long duration → higher economic damage.

---

## **7. Secondary Effects**

Natural hazards often trigger chain reactions:

* Earthquake → fire, landslides
* Flood → epidemics, water contamination
* Cyclone → storm surge, massive erosion

Secondary impacts often kill more people than the main hazard.

---

# **B. Damage Potential of Major Natural Hazards**

Now the scoring section — write this when they ask:

**“Describe the damage potential of natural hazards”**
OR
**“Explain the impact of major natural hazards.”**

---

## **1. Earthquakes**

**Damage Potential:**

* Building collapse
* Roads, bridges destruction
* Liquefaction
* Landslides
* Fires due to gas pipeline rupture
* Massive casualties (sudden onset, no warning)

Earthquakes are the deadliest because you cannot evacuate.

---

## **2. Floods**

**Damage Potential:**

* Loss of life (drowning)
* Destruction of crops
* Damage to houses and infrastructure
* Water contamination → diseases
* Economic loss due to displacement

Floods cause maximum **long-term** economic damage in India.

---

## **3. Cyclones**

**Damage Potential:**

* Extremely strong winds
* Storm surge flooding (most deadly part)
* House destruction
* Uprooting trees
* Crop devastation
* Power grid collapse

Cyclones have high warning time but cause massive physical destruction.

---

## **4. Drought**

**Damage Potential:**

* Crop failure
* Water scarcity
* Livestock deaths
* Famine-like conditions
* Migration
* Long-term economic decline

Slowest but one of the most economically devastating hazards.

---

## **5. Landslides**

**Damage Potential:**

* Burial of settlements
* Road/rail blockages
* Dam failure risk
* River blockage → flash floods

Common in Himalayan regions.

---

## **6. Heatwaves / Cold waves**

**Damage Potential:**

* High mortality among elderly, workers
* Crop stress
* Power grid overload
* Forest fire risk (heatwaves)

Heatwaves are increasing rapidly due to climate change.

---

## **7. Forest Fires**

**Damage Potential:**

* Loss of biodiversity
* Air pollution
* Property destruction
* Carbon emissions
* Soil erosion

Often triggered by heat + human negligence.

---

# **Exam-ready short answer (4–6 marks):**

Natural hazards have specific characteristics such as sudden or gradual onset, varying predictability, spatial distribution, intensity, duration, and secondary effects. Their damage potential includes destruction of infrastructure, loss of life, environmental degradation, economic losses, and long-term disruption of livelihoods. Examples include earthquakes causing building collapse, floods causing contamination and displacement, and drought causing agricultural and economic losses.
